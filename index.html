Abstract

Pulsars are neutron stars that emit very regular pulses of radiation, allowing scientists to measure
time with high accuracy. Some pulsars, especially millisecond pulsars, are extremely stable and act
as natural clocks in space. This article explains how pulsar timing works, how pulsar signals
can be combined into a time scale, how they compare with atomic clocks, and how recent research
uses both pulsars and atomic data together. Four academic sources support the discussion.

Introduction

Timekeeping is essential in science, navigation, computing, and communication systems. Atomic
clocks are currently the most accurate devices for measuring time, but they can still drift slightly over
many years. For this reason, scientists have investigated the use of pulsars as natural timekeepers.
Pulsars are dense rotating neutron stars that emit regular pulses. Millisecond pulsars rotate very
quickly and their pulses arrive with consistent timing, making them useful for long-term stability.



This article summarises four scientific studies showing how pulsars can measure time, how pulsar
time scales are created, how pulsars compare with atomic clocks, and how new research aims to
combine the strengths of both systems.

Analysis and discussion

Becker (2018) explains that pulsar timing works by predicting when a pulse should arrive and
comparing this with the actual time measured at the telescope. Factors such as interstellar gas,
relativity, and observatory clock corrections must be accounted for. When these effects are included,
the remaining differences, known as timing residuals, are small and mostly random. This shows that
pulsars can behave as long-term natural clocks. (Referenced in lines 33–55)

tobs = tmodel + R(t) 

This simple equation shows that the observed pulse time is equal to the model prediction plus a
residual term, which is small for a stable pulsar.

Hobbs et al. (2020) describe how timing data from multiple pulsars can be combined to create a
pulsar-based time scale. Averaging the timing data reduces random noise and produces a stable
long-term signal. Their work produced the pulsar time scale TT(IPTA16), which is accurate enough to
compare with international atomic time and detect slow changes in atomic clocks. (Referenced in
lines 57–80)

TPSR (t) = 1 N ∑ Ri (t) 

This equation represents a simplified pulsar time scale where the average of the timing residuals
from N pulsars is used as a stable reference signal.

Matsakis and Foster (1996) compare pulsars with groups of atomic clocks. Atomic clocks are very
accurate over short periods, such as seconds or minutes, but they can drift slightly over many years.
Pulsars, while less precise in the short term, are extremely stable over long periods. The authors show
that pulsars can act as long-term references to check whether atomic clocks drift over time.
(Referenced in lines 82–105)

Liu et al. (2023) propose combining pulsar observations with atomic clock signals to create a hybrid
time scale. In their system, pulsar data are used to correct slow drift in atomic clocks, while the atomic
clocks still provide excellent short-term accuracy. This combined approach reduces long-term errors
and may influence future international timekeeping standards. (Referenced in lines 107–135)

Conclusion

Pulsars offer an effective way to support long-term timekeeping because their pulses are stable and
predictable. By understanding and correcting pulsar timing data, scientists can treat pulsars as
natural clocks. When timing data from multiple pulsars are combined, a stable pulsar-based time
scale can be created that complements atomic clocks. Research comparing the two systems shows
that atomic clocks provide excellent short-term precision, while pulsars provide long-term stability.
Recent studies propose combining both, suggesting that the most accurate future timekeeping may
use a hybrid pulsar–atomic approach.
